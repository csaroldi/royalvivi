Buckingham Palace & Changing of Guard
▾ INFO
📜 History

The official residence of the British monarch since Queen Victoria moved in in 1837. The Changing of the Guard dates back to 1660 — the iconic red coats and bearskin hats belong to the five regiments of the Foot Guards who have protected the monarchy for centuries.

✦ Did You Know

The palace has 775 rooms, its own post office, swimming pool, cinema, and a private ATM in the basement. It is effectively a small village behind iron gates.

📍 Take Me There
St James's Park
▾ INFO
📜 History

The oldest of London's eight Royal Parks, originally landscaped by Henry VIII in 1532 as a deer park. The view of Buckingham Palace from the Blue Bridge is one of the most photographed in London.

✦ Did You Know

Pelicans have lived in the park since 1664, when the Russian Ambassador gave the first pair to King Charles II. Their descendants are still fed by the park keepers every day at 2:30pm.

📍 Take Me There
Westminster Abbey
▾ INFO
📜 History

Founded in 960 AD, this Gothic masterpiece has hosted every royal coronation since William the Conqueror in 1066 — 40 in total. Final resting place of 17 monarchs, Charles Darwin, Isaac Newton, and Geoffrey Chaucer.

✦ Did You Know

The oldest door in the UK — nearly 1,000 years old — is hidden inside the Abbey. Carbon dated to between 1032 and 1064 AD. Most visitors walk straight past it.

📍 Take Me There
Big Ben & Houses of Parliament
▾ INFO
📜 History

Big Ben is the name of the 13.7-tonne bell inside the Elizabeth Tower, not the tower itself. The bell has been chiming since 1859.

✦ Did You Know

The clock's accuracy is maintained by adding or removing old penny coins from the pendulum. One penny changes the speed by 0.4 seconds per day. Cross Westminster Bridge for the classic postcard shot.

📍 Take Me There
London Eye
▾ INFO
📜 History

Opened in 2000 as a temporary millennium structure, it proved so popular it became permanent. At 135 metres, each of the 32 capsules represents one of the London boroughs.

✦ Did You Know

A full rotation takes exactly 30 minutes. The wheel never fully stops — passengers board while it moves very slowly. On a clear day you can see Windsor Castle, 40 kilometres away.

📍 Take Me There
Leake Street Arches
▾ INFO
📜 History

A 300-metre tunnel under Waterloo station transformed into London's most famous legal graffiti space after Banksy held his Cans Festival here in 2008.

✦ Did You Know

One of the only places in London where graffiti is completely legal. The walls are entirely repainted every few weeks. Artists come from around the world to paint here.

📍 Take Me There
South Bank — The Queen's Walk
▾ INFO
📜 History

Largely derelict industrial land until the Festival of Britain in 1951 transformed it. The Queen's Walk runs between Westminster and Tower bridges — one of the great urban walks in Europe.

✦ Did You Know

The second-hand book market under Waterloo Bridge has been running since the 1980s and never closes — even in heavy rain. One of the only permanent outdoor book markets in the world.

Tower of London
▾ INFO
📜 History

Nearly 1,000 years old, the Tower has served as a royal palace, prison, zoo, armoury, and mint. Two of Henry VIII's wives were beheaded here. Home to the Crown Jewels — over 23,000 gemstones in the royal regalia.

✦ Did You Know

Six ravens must be kept at the Tower by royal decree. Legend says if they ever leave, the kingdom will fall. Each raven has its own name and a dedicated Ravenmaster.

📍 Take Me There
Tower Bridge
▾ INFO
📜 History

Completed in 1894. Frequently — and incorrectly — called London Bridge by tourists. The real London Bridge is the plain, unremarkable one just upstream.

✦ Did You Know

The two bascules weigh around 1,000 tonnes each. Walk the glass floor high-level walkways for a view straight down to the Thames.

📍 Take Me There
St Katharine Docks
▾ INFO
📜 History

Built in 1828, once the beating heart of London's trade, handling ivory, marble, spices, and wine. Now a marina filled with historic vessels.

✦ Did You Know

Built by demolishing an entire medieval neighbourhood — over 11,000 people evicted with zero compensation. Most tourists miss this gem.

📍 Take Me There
Shad Thames
▾ INFO
📜 History

One of the last remaining Victorian warehouse streets in London. The iron walkways overhead were used to transfer goods between warehouses.

✦ Did You Know

Find the arched brick doorway with the SHAD THAMES sign — Tower Bridge is perfectly framed through the arch. Most people walk straight past it.

📍 Take Me There
Borough Market
▾ INFO
📜 History

One of London's oldest food markets, with trading on this site since the 12th century. The Victorian iron-and-glass structure sits under the rail arches of London Bridge station.

✦ Did You Know

Survived demolition in the 1850s only because traders raised the money to buy the freehold. Best Thursday to Saturday. Closed Mondays.

📍 Take Me There
The Shard
▾ INFO
📜 History

At 310 metres, the tallest building in the UK. Designed by Renzo Piano, completed in 2012. Its tapered glass form was inspired by London's historic church spires.

✦ Did You Know

Rather than paying for the observation deck, go for drinks at Oblix on the upper floors at dusk. The views are essentially the same and the experience is far better.

📍 Take Me There
Millennium Bridge
▾ INFO
📜 History

London's first new Thames crossing in over a century, opened in 2000. Connects St Paul's Cathedral to the Tate Modern.

✦ Did You Know

On opening day it wobbled so badly it had to be closed immediately. After £5 million of repairs it reopened in 2002. Londoners still call it the Wobbly Bridge.

📍 Take Me There
St Paul's Cathedral
▾ INFO
📜 History

Christopher Wren's masterpiece, completed in 1710. During the Blitz it became a symbol of national defiance — photographs of its dome rising through the smoke were published worldwide.

✦ Did You Know

The Whispering Gallery inside the dome: whisper against the wall and someone on the opposite side, 34 metres away, can hear every word clearly.

📍 Take Me There
St Dunstan in the East
▾ INFO
📜 History

A medieval church bombed in the Blitz of 1941 and never rebuilt. The City turned the shell into a public garden in 1967. Vines and fig trees now grow through the Gothic arches.

✦ Did You Know

One of London's best kept secrets — tucked between office buildings. Completely silent in the middle of one of the busiest square miles on earth.

📍 Take Me There
Sky Garden
▾ INFO
📜 History

London's highest public garden on top of 20 Fenchurch Street — the Walkie-Talkie building. Free entry with a pre-booked ticket, which disappears weeks in advance.

✦ Did You Know

The building's concave glass facade concentrated sunlight so intensely in 2013 that it melted parts of a Jaguar parked on the street below.

Notting Hill & Portobello Road
▾ INFO
📜 History

Site of the 1958 race riots and birthplace of the Notting Hill Carnival — now the largest street festival in Europe. The pastel houses on Elgin Crescent are the classic photo spot.

✦ Did You Know

The pastel houses were painted different colours so that drunk residents could find their way home. Best light is in the morning before the crowds arrive.

📍 Take Me There
Kensington Palace
▾ INFO
📜 History

A royal residence since 1689. Queen Victoria was born and raised here. Princess Diana lived here until her death in 1997.

✦ Did You Know

After Diana's death in 1997, over a million bouquets were left outside the gates within a single week — one of the largest spontaneous memorials in history.

📍 Take Me There
Kensington Gardens
▾ INFO
📜 History

Separated from Hyde Park in 1728, they were the private gardens of Kensington Palace for over a century before opening to the public in 1837.

✦ Did You Know

The Peter Pan statue was installed overnight in 1912 by J.M. Barrie himself with no prior announcement — so children would believe it appeared by magic.

📍 Take Me There
Hyde Park
▾ INFO
📜 History

Originally Henry VIII's royal hunting ground, seized from Westminster Abbey monks in 1536. Site of the Great Exhibition of 1851 and Live Aid in 1985. 142 hectares.

✦ Did You Know

Speakers' Corner in the north-east has been a legal space for public oratory since 1872. Anyone can turn up and say almost anything.

📍 Take Me There
Natural History Museum
▾ INFO
📜 History

One of the world's greatest natural history collections, in a stunning Romanesque building completed in 1881. Over 80 million specimens spanning billions of years. Free entry.

✦ Did You Know

The blue whale skeleton in Hintze Hall — nicknamed Hope — is 25 metres long. Also here: moon rock, a meteorite from Mars, and a dodo specimen.

📍 Take Me There
Victoria and Albert Museum
▾ INFO
📜 History

The world's largest museum of decorative arts and design, founded in 1852. Over 2.27 million objects spanning 5,000 years. Free entry.

✦ Did You Know

The V&A holds the world's largest collection of post-classical sculpture outside Italy. The central courtyard is one of London's best coffee spots.

📍 Take Me There
Holland Park & Kyoto Garden
▾ INFO
📜 History

Built on the grounds of a Jacobean mansion largely destroyed in the Blitz of 1940. The ruins still stand. The Kyoto Garden was gifted by the city of Kyoto in 1991.

✦ Did You Know

Peacocks roam freely through the grounds. The Kyoto Garden is almost always quiet even on weekends. One of London's genuine hidden gems.

📍 Take Me There
Harrods
▾ INFO
📜 History

Founded in 1834 as a small grocer, now the world's most famous department store. The Egyptian escalator and food halls are an experience.

✦ Did You Know

There is a strict dress code — no ripped jeans, no football shirts, no swimwear. Even if you buy nothing, the food halls are worth the visit.

Windsor Castle
▾ INFO
📜 History

The oldest and largest occupied castle in the world, continuously used as a royal residence for nearly 1,000 years. Built by William the Conqueror after 1066.

✦ Did You Know

The Round Tower sits on a man-made motte built by the Normans — part of the castle still stands on an artificial hill created almost a millennium ago.

📍 Take Me There
St George's Chapel
▾ INFO
📜 History

One of the finest examples of Gothic architecture in England. Spiritual home of the Order of the Garter and burial site of 11 monarchs including Henry VIII and Queen Elizabeth II.

✦ Did You Know

It is a royal peculiar — meaning it answers directly to the monarch rather than any bishop.

📍 Take Me There
Windsor Town & Thames Walk
▾ INFO
📜 History

Dating to Saxon times, Windsor developed to support the royal household. Its streets have been shaped for centuries by the castle above.

✦ Did You Know

The main street was deliberately aligned to create a clear visual line from the castle straight down to the river — reinforcing royal presence even in the town's design.

📍 Take Me There
Radcliffe Camera & Bodleian Library
▾ INFO
📜 History

The Bodleian was founded in 1602 with over 13 million items. A legal deposit library — entitled by law to receive a free copy of every book published in the UK.

✦ Did You Know

Readers must swear an oath never to bring fire into the building. Camera comes from Latin for room — the most photographed building has nothing to do with photography.

📍 Take Me There
Christ Church College
▾ INFO
📜 History

Founded by Henry VIII in 1546. One of Oxford's most prestigious colleges and uniquely also functions as a cathedral. Alumni include Lewis Carroll and W.H. Auden.

✦ Did You Know

Its Great Hall inspired the Hogwarts dining hall. Its cathedral is one of the smallest in England, despite the college's enormous influence.

📍 Take Me There
Oxford Covered Market
▾ INFO
📜 History

Opened in 1774 to bring traders off the streets, operating continuously for over 250 years. A hub of independent businesses — no chains.

✦ Did You Know

Ben's Cookies opened here in 1983 — the original shop is still there. Perfect for a coffee before the train back to London.

British Museum
▾ INFO
📜 History

Founded in 1753, the world's first national public museum. Collection spans two million years of human history across virtually every civilisation. Free entry.

✦ Did You Know

The Rosetta Stone — the key that unlocked Egyptian hieroglyphics — is here. So are the Elgin Marbles, the Lewis Chessmen, and a 3,000-year-old mummified cat.

📍 Take Me There
Oxford Street, Regent Street & Carnaby
▾ INFO
📜 History

Oxford Street: a trading route since Roman times. Carnaby Street: epicentre of the Swinging Sixties, synonymous with mod culture and The Who.

✦ Did You Know

Oxford Street has 300 shops and ~500,000 visitors per day. Selfridges, founded in 1909, was the first department store to use window displays.

📍 Take Me There
Chinatown
▾ INFO
📜 History

Moved to Gerrard Street in the 1970s after the original in Limehouse was destroyed in the Blitz. The ornamental gates and pagoda phone boxes were added in the 1980s.

✦ Did You Know

Lunar New Year celebrations here draw hundreds of thousands. Street signs are in English and traditional Chinese. Best for dinner — the dim sum is exceptional.

📍 Take Me There
Piccadilly Circus & Leicester Square
▾ INFO
📜 History

Piccadilly Circus dates from 1819. The Shaftesbury Memorial Fountain was erected in 1893. Leicester Square: London's entertainment hub since the 1630s.

✦ Did You Know

The angel statue is not Eros — it is Anteros, the god of selfless love. Almost everyone gets this wrong. The TKTS booth sells same-day West End tickets at up to 50% off.

📍 Take Me There
Trafalgar Square & National Gallery
▾ INFO
📜 History

Built to commemorate Trafalgar 1805. Nelson's Column 52 metres. Gallery founded 1824 with 38 paintings, now over 2,300 works spanning 700 years. Free since day one.

✦ Did You Know

Van Gogh's Sunflowers here is one of only five versions. The fourth plinth was empty for 150 years because the funds ran out. Since 1999 it hosts rotating contemporary art.

📍 Take Me There
Covent Garden
▾ INFO
📜 History

London's main fruit and vegetable market from the 1650s until 1974. The Victorian market building was restored as a shopping and entertainment destination.

✦ Did You Know

The only place in London where street performers are legally licensed and auditioned. Acts range from world-class opera to comedy.

📍 Take Me There
Neal's Yard & Seven Dials
▾ INFO
📜 History

Seven Dials is a Victorian junction where seven roads converge around a sundial. Neal's Yard is a tiny hidden courtyard with vibrantly painted buildings.

✦ Did You Know

Most people walk straight past the entrance to Neal's Yard without noticing it. The colours are a deliberate contrast to the grey streets around it.

📍 Take Me There
Liberty London
▾ INFO
📜 History

The most beautiful department store in London. The Tudor building was built in the 1920s using the timbers of two old Royal Navy warships.

✦ Did You Know

The building was designed to look 400 years old when it was brand new. The Liberty print fabrics have been iconic since the Arts and Crafts movement.

📍 Take Me There
Soho & West End Show

Soho has been London's creative and countercultural heart since the 18th century. The West End theatre district has over 40 venues.

✦ Did You Know

Check TodayTix app for last-minute deals. Sunday evening shows tend to be less crowded.

Abbey Road
▾ INFO
📜 History

Opened in 1931, the world's oldest purpose-built recording studio. The Beatles recorded almost their entire catalogue here 1962-1969.

✦ Did You Know

Paul McCartney crossed barefoot, sparking rumours he had died. The crossing is now a protected heritage site with a live webcam on it 24 hours a day.

Little Venice
▾ INFO
📜 History

Where the Grand Union and Regent's canals meet. The name is attributed to Robert Browning, who lived here in the 1860s.

✦ Did You Know

Colourful narrowboats line the canals. Walk the towpath or stop at a canal-side cafe. Unexpectedly beautiful and peaceful.

Platform 9¾ — King's Cross
▾ INFO
📜 History

Opened in 1852, one of London's oldest mainline stations. Largely derelict until a 2000s regeneration transformed the area into one of the city's most vibrant districts.

✦ Did You Know

The Platform 9¾ installation has a luggage trolley disappearing into a brick wall. Queue is 20-40 min but moves quickly. The Harry Potter shop next door is a fan paradise.

Camden Market
▾ INFO
📜 History

Camden grew rapidly after the Regent's Canal opened in 1820. By the 1970s it was cheap enough for artists and musicians, giving rise to the punk and alternative scene.

✦ Did You Know

The Stables Market is in the former stables and horse hospital of a Victorian railway — the Gothic arches and cobblestones are original. A bronze Amy Winehouse statue stands near the entrance.

The view from the top is legally protected — no building can ever obstruct it. Arrive 30 minutes before sunset for the best light. This is the one.
